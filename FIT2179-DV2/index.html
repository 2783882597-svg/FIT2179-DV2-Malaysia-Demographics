Twenty-five years of population, age structure, geography, and well-being — explored across ten visualisations grouped into four chapters.

Part 01 — Growth

Population Growth

How Malaysia’s total population and growth rate have shifted across the past two decades.

Figure 01

Population has grown steadily since 2000

Malaysia’s population increased from 23.0M in 2000 to 35.6M in 2024 — a near-linear rise of roughly half a million people per year.

Figure 02

The growth rate is slowing

Annual growth has gradually declined from above 2.4% in the early 2000s to around 1.2% in 2024, with a noticeable dip during the pandemic years.

Part 02 — Structure

Population Structure

The age composition and gender balance behind the headline numbers.

Figure 03

Working-age adults form the largest group(2024)

In 2024, people aged 25–54 make up 44.6% of the population, while the 65+ share continues to climb.

Figure 04

A strong working-age base(2024)

The population pyramid bulges across the 20–44 age groups. Older bands are smaller today but will define the next two decades of planning.

Figure 05

Gender balance is remarkably stable

Since 2000, males have held a slight majority (~52%), with both shares moving by less than a single percentage point across 25 years.

Part 03 — Geography

Geographic Distribution

Where people actually live — across the states and along the urban–rural divide.

Figure 06

Population is concentrated in a few large states(2024)

The map shows that population is concentrated in Selangor, Johor and Sabah, while smaller states and federal territories such as Perlis, Putrajaya and Labuan host far fewer residents.

Figure 07

Selangor leads the state ranking(2024)

Selangor (7.36M) is well ahead of Johor (4.18M) and Sabah (3.74M) — Together, the top five states account for about three-fifths of Malaysia's population.

Figure 08

Malaysia is becoming increasingly urbanised(2024)

The urban share has risen from 62.0% in 2000 to 76.9% in 2024 — with rural population shrinking proportionally each year.

Part 04 — Insights

Additional Insights

Life expectancy and dependency trends — the slow currents that shape Malaysia’s demographic future.

Figure 09

Life expectancy has improved — with one dip(2024)

Life expectancy at birth rose from 72.7 years in 2000 to 76.8 years in 2024, briefly dropping to 73.9 during the 2021 pandemic year.

Figure 10

Dependency ratio has fallen sharply(2024)

From 61.4 dependents per 100 working-age adults in 2000 to 41.9 in 2024, the ratio suggests Malaysia had a larger working-age base over this period.
